Phonological Analysis of the "Yama-ichi-koku" Notation in the Wei Zhi

The Final Conclusion to the Yamatai Debate, Unraveled by Scientific Phonology

A revolutionary argument breaking the century-long stalemate. This paper challenges the traditional reading of "Yamatai-koku," arguing it is a historical misreading. Through a scientific analysis of the original "Yama-ichi-koku" notation using the "3rd Century Phonological Transition Model," this study proves that "ichi" (壹), with its plosive consonant, was the only rational phonetic transcription capable of accurately capturing the sound of "Yamato" in Kyushu at that time. This finding completely invalidates the phonological basis of the Kinai (Yamato region) theory, which relies on reading "Tai" (臺) as "Yamato," and scientifically supports the Yama-ichi-koku = Kyushu Yamato theory.

Japan's First Emperor Was a Chemist

Decoding the 1300-Year-Old Cipher of "Syrup-Making Without Water" in the Nihon Shoki

Discover the astonishing scientific truth hidden in Japan's foundation myth. The peculiar ritual of "syrup-making without water" performed by the first emperor, Jinmu, was not a myth but a coded technical manual describing the advanced chemical process of distilling mercury from cinnabar. Using evidence from lead isotope analysis and archaeoscience, this paper presents a new history of Japan's origin, where the Yamato dynasty established its rule not by force, but by technological supremacy.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Where was Yamatai located?
A: Through phonological analysis, this research proves that "Yama-ichi-koku" recorded in the Wei Zhi refers to Yamato in Kyushu. The Kinai (Yamato region) theory lacks phonological basis.
Q: What is the difference between Yamatai and Yama-ichi-koku?
A: "Yamatai" is a later misreading. The oldest manuscripts record "Yama-ichi-koku," which is closest to the original text. Phonological analysis proves that the plosive in "ichi" accurately captures the sound of Yamato in Kyushu.
Q: Why is phonology important?
A: It allows us to scientifically reconstruct how Chinese scribes in the 3rd century heard and transcribed Japanese place names. By reproducing pronunciation from that era rather than modern readings, we can reveal the true meaning of place names.
Q: Did Emperor Jinmu really exist?
A: This research reveals that the ritual of "syrup-making without water" recorded in the Nihon Shoki is actually an encoded description of the chemical process of distilling mercury from cinnabar, suggesting it may reflect historical fact rather than myth.
Q: What distinguishes this research from traditional studies?
A: In addition to conventional documentary history, this research integrates scientific methods such as phonology, analytical chemistry, and military geography. Arguments are developed based on scientifically verifiable evidence rather than subjective interpretation.
