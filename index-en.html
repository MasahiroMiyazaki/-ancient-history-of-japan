Phonological Analysis of the "Yamaichi-koku" Notation in Gishi Wajinden

New Horizons in the Yamatai Controversy Opened by Science

A revolutionary demonstration breaking over 100 years of stagnation in the debate. While generally read as "Yamatai-koku," recent research reveals this is a later misreading. This paper analyzes "Yamaichi-koku," found in the oldest manuscripts, using a "3rd-century Phonological Shift Model." The results demonstrate that the stop sound contained in "Ichi" (壹) is the only phonologically rational transcription that accurately captures the sound of "Yamato" (Mountain Gate) in Kyushu at that time. Conversely, "Tai" (臺) does not phonologically hold up as a transcription of "Yamato" given 3rd-century phonology (sounds closer to "la" or "dwe"). This paper strongly supports the theory that Yamaichi-koku was located in Yamato (Yamon), Kyushu.

The First Emperor Was a Chemist

Deciphering the 1,300-Year Mystery of "Musui-zo-ame" in Nihon Shoki

The surprising truth of scientific technology hidden within Japan's founding mythology. The strange ritual of "Musui-zo-ame" (Waterless Candy Making) performed by the first Emperor, Jimmu, was not a myth but an "encrypted technical manual" describing a sophisticated chemical process for refining mercury (a strategic material) from cinnabar. By utilizing lead isotope analysis and archaeological science, this paper presents a new historical image overturning the origins of Japan, suggesting the Yamato sovereignty was built on technological hegemony rather than mere military force.

FAQ
Q: Where was Yamatai located?
A: This research strongly supports the theory that "Yamaichi-koku" in the Gishi Wajinden refers to "Yamato" (Yamon) in Kyushu, based on phonological analysis. Conversely, the "Tai" notation used as the basis for the Kinai theory is difficult to explain given 3rd-century phonology.
Q: What is the difference between Yamatai and Yamaichi?
A: "Yamatai" is a later misreading. The oldest and most reliable manuscripts write "Yamaichi-koku" (邪馬壹國), which is the notation closest to the original text. Phonological analysis proves that the stop sound in "Ichi" accurately captures the sound of "Yamato" (Mountain Gate) in Kyushu.
Q: Why is phonology important?
A: It allows us to scientifically reconstruct how Chinese scribes heard Japanese place names and which characters they used to transcribe them in the 3rd century. By reproducing the pronunciation of that era rather than modern readings, the true meaning of place names is revealed.
Q: Did Emperor Jimmu actually exist?
A: This research reveals that the "Musui-zo-ame" ritual described in the Nihon Shoki is an encryption of an actual chemical process (refining mercury from cinnabar). This suggests the accounts reflect historical facts rather than mere mythology.
Q: How does this differ from traditional research?
A: In addition to traditional textual history, this research introduces STEM methods such as phonology, analytical chemistry, and military geography. Arguments are developed based on scientifically verifiable evidence rather than subjective interpretation.
